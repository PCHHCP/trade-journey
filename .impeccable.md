## Design Context

### Users
Tyche serves individual traders who use the product to record trades, review execution discipline, analyze performance, and archive trading data over time. The product is used in a self-directed context where the user wants a clear, trustworthy workspace for journaling and reflection rather than a social, speculative, or entertainment-driven experience.

### Brand Personality
The brand should feel personal, intuitive, and cool without becoming flashy. The interface should evoke professionalism, motivation, confidence, and calm. Its tone should support disciplined decision-making and post-trade reflection, helping users feel focused and in control.

### Aesthetic Direction
The current product already leans toward a technical, motion-aware trading interface with strong typography, dashboards, and global market cues. Evolve that direction into something more unified and mature: professional, cinematic, and high-signal, but never noisy. Reference inspiration includes `ultratrader.app` and `tradezella`, especially for polished trading-product energy and focused information presentation. Avoid anything that feels like a crypto gambling dashboard, a generic SaaS template, or an overly neon aesthetic. Move away from the current blue/green mix toward a more cohesive palette while preserving contrast, clarity, and a premium feel. Support both light and dark themes unless a future product decision narrows that scope.

### Design Principles
1. Design for disciplined trading, not speculation theater. Every screen should reinforce execution review, performance understanding, and data stewardship.
2. Lead with clarity and hierarchy. Important numbers, states, and actions should read instantly without visual clutter or decorative overload.
3. Keep the interface cool and distinctive, but restrained. Use bold typography, strong composition, and purposeful detail without drifting into neon, hype, or gimmicks.
4. Make data feel trustworthy. Charts, tables, imports, and journaling flows should look precise, stable, and intentional.
5. Use motion as guidance, not spectacle. Animations should add confidence, pacing, and polish while staying controlled and professional.
